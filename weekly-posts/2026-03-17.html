Unified Scaling Laws for Turbulent Premixed Flames

🔥

The phenomenon: Researchers at Yale and Princeton have discovered that turbulent premixed flames don't follow the same scaling laws when you switch from methane to hydrogen. The culprit? Thermodiffusive instability.

What's happening: While methane flames (Lewis numbers ≈ 1) behave predictably, hydrogen flames with their low Lewis number develop thermodiffusive instability—heat and mass diffuse at different rates, causing the flame to wrinkle and stretch in unexpected ways.

Key finding:

Traditional flame scaling laws fail at K < 10. Below this, thermodiffusive effects dominate and flames don't fit the unity-Lewis-number models we've used for decades.

Why it matters: As the world pivots to hydrogen for clean combustion, engineers need predictive models that actually work. This research validates that turbulent hydrogen flames need their own set of scaling laws—critical for designing safe H₂ engines and turbines.
